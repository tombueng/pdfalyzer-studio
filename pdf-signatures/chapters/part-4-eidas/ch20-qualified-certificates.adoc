= Qualified Certificates & Trust Services

[.lead]
A certificate is just a data structure -- some ASN.1 fields, a public key, and a signature from someone who claims to be a Certificate Authority.
What makes a certificate _qualified_ is not a technical property you can detect by parsing bytes alone.
It is a legal status, conferred by a government supervisory body, published in a machine-readable trust infrastructure, and backed by an institutional apparatus that most developers have never heard of, let alone queried programmatically.

This chapter takes you through that entire apparatus: the Qualified Trust Service Providers that issue qualified certificates, the EU Trusted List that publishes who they are, the technical requirements that separate a qualified certificate from an ordinary one, the QSCD hardware that protects the keys, and the Java code you need to actually parse and verify all of this.
If Chapter 19 was the legal framework, this chapter is the plumbing.

== Qualified Trust Service Providers (QTSPs)

=== What a QTSP Is

A **Qualified Trust Service Provider** is an organization that has been audited, approved, and listed by a national supervisory body within the EU as meeting the requirements of {eidas-reg} Articles 20-24.
The word "qualified" is doing serious work here -- it is not a marketing term, not a self-declaration, and not something you obtain by installing software.
It is a legal status, analogous to a bank receiving a banking license: you cannot call yourself a QTSP unless a government body says you are one.

A QTSP can provide one or more qualified trust services:

- **Qualified certificates for electronic signatures** (natural persons)
- **Qualified certificates for electronic seals** (legal persons)
- **Qualified timestamps** (RFC 3161 tokens from a qualified TSA)
- **Qualified electronic delivery services** (certified email equivalents)
- **Qualified website authentication certificates** (the controversial Article 45, rewritten in eIDAS 2.0)
- **Qualified electronic attestations of attributes** (new in {eidas2-reg})

The qualification is _per-service_, not per-organization.
A provider might be qualified for timestamps but not for certificates, or for seals but not for signatures.
This matters when you are parsing the EU Trusted List -- you need to check not just whether the provider is listed, but _which services_ are qualified.

=== How to Become a QTSP

The path to becoming a QTSP is neither quick nor cheap.
Here is the process, simplified but accurate:

1. **Establish the infrastructure.** You need HSMs (certified to Common Criteria EAL4+ or equivalent), a secure data center (typically ISO 27001 certified), redundant systems, trained personnel, and documented processes for every aspect of certificate lifecycle management -- issuance, suspension, revocation, key generation, key destruction.

2. **Write a Certificate Practice Statement (CPS).** This is the document that describes, in exhaustive detail, how you operate: identity verification procedures, key management, certificate profiles, revocation handling, disaster recovery, incident response.
A CPS for a QTSP is typically 80-150 pages long.

3. **Get a conformity assessment.** An accredited **Conformity Assessment Body (CAB)** evaluates your infrastructure, processes, and CPS against {eidas-reg} and the applicable ETSI standards (primarily ETSI EN 319 401 for general TSP requirements and ETSI EN 319 411-1/411-2 for certificate issuance).
The assessment produces a **Conformity Assessment Report (CAR)**.

4. **Submit to the national supervisory body.** Each EU member state designates a supervisory body for QTSPs: in Germany, the **Bundesnetzagentur** (Federal Network Agency); in France, **ANSSI**; in Italy, **AgID** (Agenzia per l'Italia Digitale).
You submit the CAR, your CPS, and supporting documentation.

5. **Supervisory body review and approval.** The supervisory body reviews everything, may request additional information or on-site inspections, and eventually grants or denies qualified status.

6. **Listing on the national Trusted List.** Once approved, the supervisory body adds your service(s) to the national **Trusted Service Status List (TSL)** -- the country's portion of the EU Trusted List.
Your qualified status takes effect when the updated TSL is published.

7. **Ongoing supervision.** Qualification is not a one-time event.
{eidas-reg} Article 20(1) mandates audits at least every 24 months.
{eidas-reg} Article 19(2) requires QTSPs to report security incidents to the supervisory body within 24 hours.

[WARNING]
====
The entire process from initial infrastructure setup to EUTL listing typically takes **18-36 months** and costs **several hundred thousand to several million euros**, depending on the scope of services and the jurisdiction.
This is by design: the barrier to entry ensures that only well-resourced, professionally operated organizations can issue qualified certificates.

If someone offers you "qualified certificates" from a provider that is not on the EUTL, they are either confused about what "qualified" means or deliberately misleading you.
Always verify against the EUTL.
====

=== The Supervision Model

The eIDAS supervision model is a two-layer structure:

**National layer:** Each member state's supervisory body is responsible for QTSPs established in its territory.
The supervisory body grants and revokes qualified status, maintains the national TSL, conducts or commissions audits, handles incident reports, and imposes sanctions for non-compliance.

**EU layer:** The European Commission maintains the **List of Trusted Lists (LOTL)** -- a master document that points to every national TSL.
The Commission does not supervise individual QTSPs; it supervises the supervisory bodies indirectly, through the mutual evaluation mechanism and through the eIDAS Expert Group.

.Supervision hierarchy
[cols="1,3,3", options="header"]
|===
| Level | Entity | Responsibility

| EU
| European Commission
| Maintains LOTL, sets technical standards via implementing acts, coordinates mutual evaluations

| National
| Supervisory Body (Bundesnetzagentur, ANSSI, AgID, etc.)
| Grants/revokes qualified status, maintains national TSL, audits QTSPs, handles incidents

| Provider
| QTSP (DigiCert, D-Trust, InfoCert, etc.)
| Operates trust services, issues certificates, complies with ETSI standards, reports incidents
|===

== The EU Trusted List (EUTL)

=== Architecture: The LOT Hierarchy

The EUTL is not a single list -- it is a **hierarchy of cryptographically signed XML documents**:

1. The **LOTL (List of Trusted Lists)** is the root.
Maintained by the European Commission, published at a well-known URL.
The LOTL contains pointers (URLs) to every national TSL, along with the certificates used to verify each national TSL's signature.

2. Each **national TSL (Trusted Service Status List)** lists the trust service providers in that country, their services, the status of each service (granted, withdrawn, deprecated), and the CA certificates associated with each service.

3. Each TSL is **digitally signed** using an XML Advanced Electronic Signature (XAdES) -- the same trust-no-one verification model applied to the trust infrastructure itself.

The LOTL URL is:

----
https://ec.europa.eu/tools/lotl/eu-lotl.xml
----

[NOTE]
====
This URL has been stable for years, but production systems should make it configurable.
The official reference for the LOTL location is Commission Implementing Decision (EU) 2015/1505.
The LOTL itself contains a `NextUpdate` field indicating when the next version will be published.
National TSLs are updated on varying schedules -- some monthly, some quarterly, some whenever a change occurs.
====

=== XML Schema

The TSL XML schema is defined in ETSI TS 119 612 (replacing the older ETSI TS 102 231).
The schema is verbose, deeply nested, and frankly exhausting to read -- but it is well-structured and consistent.

=== Key Service Types and Statuses

.EUTL service type identifiers for PDF-relevant services
[cols="3,4", options="header"]
|===
| Service Type URI | Meaning

| `.../Svctype/CA/QC`
| CA issuing **qualified certificates** (check qualifiers for esign/eseal/web)

| `.../Svctype/TSA/QTST`
| **Qualified timestamp** authority

| `.../Svctype/Certstatus/OCSP/QC`
| **Qualified OCSP** responder

| `.../Svctype/Certstatus/CRL/QC`
| **Qualified CRL** issuer
|===

.EUTL service status values
[cols="3,4", options="header"]
|===
| Status URI | Meaning

| `.../Svcstatus/granted`
| Currently qualified -- this is what you are looking for

| `.../Svcstatus/withdrawn`
| Qualification has been withdrawn

| `.../Svcstatus/recognisedatnationallevel`
| Not EU-qualified, but recognized under national law

| `.../Svcstatus/deprecated`
| Service deprecated by the supervisory body
|===

[IMPORTANT]
====
A common mistake in EUTL parsing is to check only the current status.
You also need to check the **service history**.
A service that was `granted` from 2019-01-01 to 2023-06-30 and then `withdrawn` was qualified during that window.
A certificate issued during the `granted` period is still a qualified certificate, even though the service is now withdrawn.

The TSL includes a `ServiceHistory` element with `ServiceHistoryInstance` entries recording every status change.
Your validation logic must check whether the certificate was issued (and the signature created) during a period when the service status was `granted`.
====

=== Cryptographic Signing of the TSL

Every TSL (including the LOTL) is signed with an XAdES signature.
To trust the contents, you must:

1. **Verify the XML signature** on the TSL document
2. **Verify the signing certificate** against the LOTL (for national TSLs) or against the Commission's well-known certificate (for the LOTL itself)
3. **Check freshness** -- the TSL must not be past its `NextUpdate` date (or not past it by more than a configurable grace period)

The LOTL signing certificate is published by the European Commission at a well-known URL.
When the signing certificate rotates, the new certificate is announced in advance in the LOTL itself -- this is the **pivot mechanism**, and it is one of the trickiest parts of EUTL parsing to implement correctly.

[TIP]
====
For production systems, implement a scheduled refresh -- daily or weekly -- that re-downloads, re-verifies, and re-parses the entire EUTL hierarchy.
The EU DSS library (Chapter 23) provides a `TrustedListsCertificateSource` that handles all of this automatically, including caching, refresh scheduling, signature verification, and the pivot mechanism.
====

== Qualified Certificates: What Makes Them Qualified

A certificate is "qualified" when three conditions are simultaneously true:

1. It is issued by a CA whose certificate issuance service has `granted` status in the EUTL
2. The certificate contains specific technical indicators (the QCStatements extension)
3. The CA followed the procedures defined in its CPS, which was approved during the conformity assessment

Condition 3 is not something you can verify programmatically -- it is an institutional guarantee, backed by the supervisory body's ongoing oversight.
Conditions 1 and 2 are what your code checks.

=== The QCStatements Extension

The technical indicator of a qualified certificate is the **QCStatements extension** (OID `1.3.6.1.5.5.7.1.3`), defined in ETSI EN 319 412-5.
This extension contains a sequence of statements, each identified by an OID:

.QCStatements OIDs relevant to PDF signatures
[cols="3,2,4", options="header"]
|===
| OID | Short Name | Meaning

| `0.4.0.1862.1.1`
| id-etsi-qcs-QcCompliance
| This certificate is issued as a **qualified certificate** under {eidas-reg}

| `0.4.0.1862.1.4`
| id-etsi-qcs-QcSSCD
| The private key resides on a **Qualified Signature/Seal Creation Device**

| `0.4.0.1862.1.6`
| id-etsi-qcs-QcType
| The type: esign (`...6.1` = signature), eseal (`...6.2` = seal), web (`...6.3`)

| `0.4.0.1862.1.3`
| id-etsi-qcs-QcRetentionPeriod
| How long the CA retains registration information after certificate expiry

| `0.4.0.1862.1.5`
| id-etsi-qcs-QcPDS
| URL of the PKI Disclosure Statement (human-readable CPS summary)

| `0.4.0.1862.1.2`
| id-etsi-qcs-QcLimitValue
| Monetary transaction limit (rarely used)
|===

[WARNING]
====
Do **not** trust the QCStatements extension alone.
Anyone can put `id-etsi-qcs-QcCompliance` in a self-signed certificate.
The QCStatements assertion is only meaningful when the issuing CA is listed in the EUTL with `granted` status for a `CA/QC` service.

The correct validation logic is:

1. Check that the issuing CA's certificate matches one in the EUTL with `ServiceTypeIdentifier` of `CA/QC` and status `granted`
2. Check the period: the certificate's `notBefore` date must fall within the service's `granted` status period
3. _Then_ check QCStatements for the specific type (esign, eseal, web)
4. _Then_ check for QcSSCD if you need to verify QSCD usage

Steps 1-2 without steps 3-4: you know the CA is qualified, but not necessarily this specific certificate.
Steps 3-4 without steps 1-2: you have a self-asserted claim with no institutional backing.
You need all four.
====

=== Certificate Policy OIDs

Qualified certificates also carry specific **Certificate Policy** OIDs in the `certificatePolicies` extension (OID `2.5.29.32`), defined in ETSI EN 319 411-2:

.Certificate Policy OIDs for qualified certificates
[cols="3,4", options="header"]
|===
| OID | Meaning

| `0.4.0.194112.1.2`
| QCP-n: Qualified Certificate Policy for **natural persons** (signatures)

| `0.4.0.194112.1.3`
| QCP-l: Qualified Certificate Policy for **legal persons** (seals)

| `0.4.0.194112.1.1`
| QCP-n-qscd: Natural person certificates with QSCD

| `0.4.0.194112.1.4`
| QCP-l-qscd: Legal person certificates with QSCD

| `0.4.0.194112.1.0`
| QCP-w: Website authentication
|===

The `-qscd` variants indicate that the CA has verified that the subscriber's private key is on a QSCD.
This is a stronger assertion than the QcSSCD statement alone -- the policy OID says "the CA has verified the key is on a QSCD according to the procedures defined in ETSI EN 319 411-2."

=== QSCD Requirements

A Qualified Signature Creation Device (or Qualified Seal Creation Device -- QSCD covers both) must meet the requirements of {eidas-reg} Annex II:

1. The private key must be generated on the device and never leave it
2. The signing operation must occur on the device itself
3. Access to the private key must require authentication of the legitimate holder (PIN, biometric, etc.)
4. The private key cannot be derived from signatures or the public key with reasonable assurance

**Certification path:** Common Criteria evaluation (ISO/IEC 15408) at EAL4+ against Protection Profiles defined in EN 419 211 (for smart cards and tokens) or EN 419 221 (for HSMs and remote signing).
A national certification body (BSI in Germany, ANSSI in France) performs the evaluation.

**Hardware categories in practice:**

- **Smart cards** (Thales IDPrime MD, Atos CardOS): private key generated on chip, never exported, signing performed on-card
- **USB tokens** (SafeNet eToken 5110, YubiKey 5 FIPS): same security model as smart cards, USB form factor
- **HSMs** (Thales Luna, Utimaco SecurityServer): rack-mounted devices for server-side signing; basis for remote signing (Chapter 22)

[CAUTION]
====
Not every HSM is a certified QSCD.
An HSM may be FIPS 140-2 Level 3 or Common Criteria EAL4+ certified for _general cryptographic operations_ without being certified as a QSCD under the specific EN 419 211/221 Protection Profiles.
The QSCD certification must reference the eIDAS-specific Protection Profiles.
====

== Real-World QTSPs

When you are debugging why a particular qualified certificate does or does not validate, knowing who issued it and where they are listed is invaluable.
Here are the QTSPs you are most likely to encounter:

**D-Trust GmbH** (Germany) -- a subsidiary of the Bundesdruckerei (the organization that prints German passports).
Listed in the German TSL, supervised by the Bundesnetzagentur.
Dominant in German government and enterprise contexts.

**DigiCert / QuoVadis** (Netherlands, Belgium) -- DigiCert acquired QuoVadis in 2019, inheriting QTSP status in multiple EU countries.
One of the few providers with both AATL and eIDAS qualified status -- useful when documents must validate in both Adobe Acrobat and European government systems.

**GlobalSign** (Belgium) -- listed as a QTSP in Belgium, supervised by the FPS Economy.
Strong enterprise presence.
AATL membership plus EUTL listing gives broad compatibility.

**Swisscom Trust Services** (Austria, Switzerland) -- the Swiss telecoms giant operates a QTSP listed in the _Austrian_ TSL (not Swiss -- because Switzerland is not in the EU) plus ZertES qualification in Switzerland.
Their remote signing service is popular for high-volume enterprise signing.

**InfoCert** (Italy) -- one of the largest European QTSPs by volume.
Listed in the Italian TSL, supervised by AgID.
Part of the Tinexta Group.
Deeply embedded in Italian public administration.

**Asseco Data Systems / Certum** (Poland) -- the dominant QTSP in Central/Eastern Europe.
If you encounter qualified certificates from Polish organizations, Certum is the likely issuer.

This list is not exhaustive -- there are over 200 qualified trust services listed across the EUTL.

== Java Code: Parsing the EUTL

=== Downloading and Parsing the LOTL

[source,java]
----
import java.io.InputStream;
import java.net.URI;
import java.util.ArrayList;
import java.util.List;

import javax.xml.parsers.DocumentBuilderFactory;

import org.w3c.dom.Document;
import org.w3c.dom.Element;
import org.w3c.dom.NodeList;

import lombok.Data;
import lombok.extern.slf4j.Slf4j;

/**
 * Parses the EU List of Trusted Lists (LOTL) to extract
 * pointers to each national Trusted Service Status List.
 */
@Slf4j
public class EutlLotlParser {

    private static final String LOTL_URL =
            "https://ec.europa.eu/tools/lotl/eu-lotl.xml";
    private static final String TSL_NS =
            "http://uri.etsi.org/02231/v2#";

    @Data
    public static class TslPointer {
        private String countryCode;
        private String tslUrl;
    }

    public List<TslPointer> parseLotl() throws Exception {
        log.info("Downloading LOTL from: {}", LOTL_URL);

        DocumentBuilderFactory factory =
                DocumentBuilderFactory.newInstance();
        factory.setNamespaceAware(true);
        // Prevent XXE attacks
        factory.setFeature(
                "http://apache.org/xml/features/"
                + "disallow-doctype-decl", true);

        Document doc;
        try (InputStream in = URI.create(LOTL_URL).toURL()
                .openStream()) {
            doc = factory.newDocumentBuilder().parse(in);
        }

        List<TslPointer> pointers = new ArrayList<>();
        NodeList nodes = doc.getElementsByTagNameNS(
                TSL_NS, "OtherTSLPointer");

        for (int i = 0; i < nodes.getLength(); i++) {
            Element pointer = (Element) nodes.item(i);

            NodeList locations = pointer.getElementsByTagNameNS(
                    TSL_NS, "TSLLocation");
            if (locations.getLength() == 0) {
                continue;
            }
            String url = locations.item(0).getTextContent().trim();

            NodeList territories = pointer.getElementsByTagNameNS(
                    TSL_NS, "SchemeTerritory");
            String country = territories.getLength() > 0
                    ? territories.item(0).getTextContent().trim()
                    : "UNKNOWN";

            TslPointer p = new TslPointer();
            p.setCountryCode(country);
            p.setTslUrl(url);
            pointers.add(p);

            log.debug("Found TSL: country={}, url={}", country, url);
        }

        log.info("Parsed LOTL: {} national TSL pointers",
                pointers.size());
        return pointers;
    }
}
----

=== Matching Certificates Against the EUTL

[source,java]
----
import java.io.ByteArrayInputStream;
import java.io.InputStream;
import java.net.URI;
import java.security.cert.CertificateFactory;
import java.security.cert.X509Certificate;
import java.util.ArrayList;
import java.util.Arrays;
import java.util.Base64;
import java.util.List;

import javax.xml.parsers.DocumentBuilderFactory;

import org.w3c.dom.Document;
import org.w3c.dom.Element;
import org.w3c.dom.NodeList;

import lombok.Data;
import lombok.extern.slf4j.Slf4j;

/**
 * Parses a national TSL and matches signer certificates
 * against qualified CA services listed in the EUTL.
 */
@Slf4j
public class EutlCertificateMatcher {

    private static final String TSL_NS =
            "http://uri.etsi.org/02231/v2#";

    @Data
    public static class QualifiedCaEntry {
        private String providerName;
        private String status;
        private X509Certificate caCertificate;
    }

    /**
     * Downloads a national TSL and extracts all CA/QC services
     * with "granted" status.
     */
    public List<QualifiedCaEntry> parseNationalTsl(String tslUrl)
            throws Exception {
        log.info("Parsing national TSL: {}", tslUrl);

        DocumentBuilderFactory factory =
                DocumentBuilderFactory.newInstance();
        factory.setNamespaceAware(true);
        factory.setFeature(
                "http://apache.org/xml/features/"
                + "disallow-doctype-decl", true);

        Document doc;
        try (InputStream in = URI.create(tslUrl).toURL()
                .openStream()) {
            doc = factory.newDocumentBuilder().parse(in);
        }

        List<QualifiedCaEntry> entries = new ArrayList<>();
        NodeList services = doc.getElementsByTagNameNS(
                TSL_NS, "TSPService");
        CertificateFactory cf =
                CertificateFactory.getInstance("X.509");

        for (int i = 0; i < services.getLength(); i++) {
            Element svc = (Element) services.item(i);

            // Check service type
            NodeList typeNodes = svc.getElementsByTagNameNS(
                    TSL_NS, "ServiceTypeIdentifier");
            if (typeNodes.getLength() == 0) {
                continue;
            }
            String type = typeNodes.item(0)
                    .getTextContent().trim();
            if (!type.contains("CA/QC")) {
                continue;
            }

            // Check status
            NodeList statusNodes = svc.getElementsByTagNameNS(
                    TSL_NS, "ServiceStatus");
            if (statusNodes.getLength() == 0) {
                continue;
            }
            String status = statusNodes.item(0)
                    .getTextContent().trim();
            if (!status.contains("granted")) {
                continue;
            }

            // Extract CA certificate
            NodeList certNodes = svc.getElementsByTagNameNS(
                    TSL_NS, "X509Certificate");
            if (certNodes.getLength() == 0) {
                continue;
            }
            String b64 = certNodes.item(0).getTextContent()
                    .replaceAll("\\s", "");
            byte[] certBytes = Base64.getDecoder().decode(b64);
            X509Certificate cert = (X509Certificate)
                    cf.generateCertificate(
                        new ByteArrayInputStream(certBytes));

            QualifiedCaEntry entry = new QualifiedCaEntry();
            entry.setStatus(status);
            entry.setCaCertificate(cert);
            entries.add(entry);

            log.info("Found qualified CA: {}",
                    cert.getSubjectX500Principal());
        }

        return entries;
    }

    /**
     * Checks whether the given signer certificate was issued
     * by a CA listed in the EUTL with "granted" status.
     */
    public boolean isIssuedByQualifiedCa(
            X509Certificate signerCert,
            X509Certificate issuerCert,
            List<QualifiedCaEntry> eutlEntries) {
        byte[] issuerEncoded;
        try {
            issuerEncoded = issuerCert.getEncoded();
        } catch (Exception e) {
            log.error("Failed to encode issuer cert", e);
            return false;
        }

        for (QualifiedCaEntry entry : eutlEntries) {
            try {
                byte[] eutlEncoded =
                        entry.getCaCertificate().getEncoded();
                if (Arrays.equals(issuerEncoded, eutlEncoded)) {
                    log.info("Issuer matched EUTL entry: {}",
                            entry.getCaCertificate()
                                .getSubjectX500Principal());
                    return true;
                }
            } catch (Exception e) {
                log.warn("Failed to encode EUTL cert", e);
            }
        }
        return false;
    }
}

[CAUTION]
====
The code above is educational -- it demonstrates the mechanics of EUTL parsing and certificate matching.
A production implementation must also:

- **Verify the XML signatures** on the LOTL and every national TSL
- **Handle the LOTL pivot mechanism** when the signing certificate rotates
- **Parse service history** to support time-based qualification checks
- **Cache and refresh** the hierarchy on a schedule (not on every validation)
- **Parse QCStatements** from the signer's certificate to determine the qualification type

For production use, the **EU DSS library** (Chapter 23) provides `TrustedListsCertificateSource`, which handles all of this correctly.
Do not reimplement this from scratch unless you have a very specific reason.
====

== Key Takeaways

- A **QTSP** is not a self-declared status -- it requires conformity assessment, supervisory body approval, and listing in the EUTL; the process takes 18-36 months and significant investment
- The **EUTL** is a hierarchy: the LOTL (Commission) points to national TSLs (supervisory bodies); every document is cryptographically signed with XAdES
- A certificate is qualified when: its issuer is in the EUTL with `granted` status for `CA/QC`, _and_ the certificate contains the `QcCompliance` QCStatement
- **QCStatements** carry machine-readable qualification data: QcCompliance, QcSSCD, QcType (esign/eseal/web)
- **Certificate Policy OIDs** from ETSI EN 319 411-2 encode the qualification policy: QCP-n, QCP-l, with `-qscd` variants
- **QSCDs** require Common Criteria EAL4+ certification against EN 419 211/221 Protection Profiles -- not every HSM qualifies
- Check **service history** when validating: a certificate issued during a `granted` period is qualified even if the service is now `withdrawn`
- Real-world QTSPs include D-Trust (DE), DigiCert/QuoVadis (NL/BE), GlobalSign (BE), Swisscom (AT/CH), InfoCert (IT), Certum (PL), and over 200 others
- For production EUTL validation, use the EU DSS framework's `TrustedListsCertificateSource` -- do not reimplement the parsing, pivot handling, and signature verification from scratch
